About

The Vespa Club of Canada is a non-profit club dedicated to the preservation of the Vespa scooter and proud to be a member of the Vespa World Club.

Canada has a history of scootering from the West coast to the Maritimes and all points in between. During the early 80's major cities like Montreal, Toronto, Hamilton, Calgary and Vancouver played roles in the popularity of Vespas courtesy of The Who's film Quadrophenia.

There were different groups that rode Vespas from Mods, skinheads, uni and of course scooter boys (Canada's version). There were many rallies in Southern Ontario, specifically Niagara Falls, and all parts in between as well as Mod nights in Toronto, Montreal and Vancouver.

It's the enthusiasm of Vespa lovers/owners that make this community thrive. Whether it's a slick paint scheme, modded out scoots, go-fast scoots or bog standard it's the rider that makes it their own.

Toronto, Montreal, Calgary and Halifax have clubs that continue to hold rallies as well as Vancouver, with their mild winters allowing year-round scootering. As the scooter culture grew across the nation, local clubs quickly formed to better meet the needs of their local scootering community.

History

The Vespa has had a long and glorious history full of ups and downs. Today there is a resurcance in intests in the Vespa. Take a walk through the history of the Vespa.

  • 1880

    Before Vespa

    Piaggio (Vespa's parent company) is formed and starts making trains and passenger cars.

  • 1940

    Piaggio makes fighter plans

    Piaggio is commissioned by the Italian government to supply their air force with fighter planes. The Allies bomb the plants in WWII. After the war Italy was forced to cease manufacturing fighter planes.

  • 1946

    The Vespa (Wasp) is born

    Aeronautical engineer, Corradinio D’Ascanio took a design he was working on for a inexpensive scooter offers it to Piaggio who needed a way to transport works to and from work. It is called the Vespa.

  • 1950 to 1960

    Vespas shipped to Canada

    Vespas shipped to Canada via navy ships during the 50's and 60's to various ports then travelling to different parts of Canada.

  • 1970

    SEARS Allstate

    In 1970 the North American market SEARS Co. badged the Canadian/US Vespa as the SEARS Allstate.

  • 1980

    The Mod Movement

    Popularity booms in part becuase of the The Who film Quadrophenia. Several scooter groups form: Mods, Skinheads, Uni and Scooter Boys (Canada's version). There are many rallies held in Southern Ontario, specifically Niagara Falls, and all parts in between. As well Toronto, Montreal and Vancouver hold Mod Nights.

  • 1981

    Vespas leave North American market

    Piaggio leaves the North American market due to strict American emission laws. Closing some shops across Canada and the USA.

  • 1985

    Many Vespa shops close across North America

    Many owners are left with out mechanical support. Some shops across Canada had left-over parts but are find. Owners turned to the UK magazine SCOOTERING for mail order parts.

  • 1990's

    Keeping the spirit alive

    In Vancouver and Toronto kept the spirit of scooter culture alive during the long darkness. Soon a sales and interest in Vespas started to grown.

  • 2001

    Piaggio returns to North America

    The Piaggio Scooter Corporation, Vespa Canada and Vespa America brought in the ET2 and ET4 models that were louder and faster. Scooters started to become more popular again.

  • Today

    New and vintage Vespas on the road

    There are many Vespa dealers across Canada and small businesses that do maintenance and restorations on Vespas. It's important to our Vespa community to support these local shops.
